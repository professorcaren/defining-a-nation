# Interests, Not Positions: A Guide for the Governors General at Simla

Your job is to broker an agreement among factions with incompatible demands and unequal power. The single most useful distinction you can learn is the difference between **positions** and **interests**.

- A **position** is a concrete demand: "India must be partitioned into two sovereign states," or "Untouchables must have separate electorates."
- An **interest** is the underlying need driving that demand: security, representation, dignity, economic opportunity, survival.

Positions clash head-on. Interests can often be satisfied simultaneously -- or at least traded. Your leverage as chair is that you control the conversation. Use that power to dig beneath the surface.

---

## Three Techniques, with Examples from Simla

### 1. Ask "Why Does That Matter to You?"

When a faction states a position, don't argue with it. Ask what's behind it. Then ask again.

**Example: Jinnah demands Pakistan -- two sovereign states, no compromise.**

His *position* is partition. But his *interests* are more specific than that. He watched Congress sweep the 1937 elections, then impose Hindi, sing *Bande Mataram* in schools, and marginalize Muslim representatives in the provinces they governed. He watched Nehru declare the League irrelevant. His core interest is that one hundred million Muslims must never be a permanent, outvotable minority in a Hindu-dominated state. He needs structural guarantees that Muslim-majority provinces cannot be governed from Delhi against their will.

If you can surface that interest, the conversation shifts. Instead of "partition yes or no," it becomes: "What kind of federal structure would give Muslim-majority provinces enough autonomy and enough veto power that they don't need full sovereignty to feel safe?" That's a question Nehru might actually engage with, because Congress could accept strong provincial autonomy more easily than it could accept losing half the country.

**Try asking:** "Mr. Jinnah, you've said that constitutional safeguards within a united India are insufficient. Help me understand specifically what failed in 1937 -- what would a structure need to look like for those safeguards to have teeth?"

---

### 2. Name the Shared Interest Out Loud

Factions that seem opposed often share interests they haven't articulated. Naming those shared interests creates common ground where none appeared to exist.

**Example: Nehru and Ambedkar are at odds over separate electorates for Untouchables.**

Their *positions* are incompatible: Nehru and Congress oppose separate electorates as divisive; Ambedkar insists they are the only guarantee of genuine Dalit political power. Gandhi nearly fasted to death to block them in 1932, and Ambedkar has never forgiven it.

But their underlying *interests* overlap more than either would admit. Both want a democratic India where every citizen has meaningful political representation. Both oppose the feudal order -- Nehru wants to abolish the *zamindari* system; Ambedkar wants to destroy the caste system. Both believe that the new India must be built on equality, not hierarchy. The real disagreement is about *trust*: Ambedkar has watched upper-caste Hindus promise equality for three thousand years and deliver nothing. He doesn't believe Congress will protect Dalits once the British leave.

**Try saying:** "It seems to me that both Congress and the Scheduled Caste Federation agree that no democratic India can be built on caste hierarchy. You disagree about the mechanism -- whether joint or separate electorates better serve that goal. But you share the conviction that Dalits must have real political power, not symbolic representation. Can we start from that shared commitment and work out what 'real power' requires in practice?"

This reframe isolates the technical disagreement (joint vs. separate electorates) from the moral agreement (Dalits must have genuine representation), making it harder for either side to treat the other as an enemy.

---

### 3. Reframe Demands as Problems to Solve

When someone makes a non-negotiable demand, translate it into a problem statement. Problems invite creative solutions; demands invite resistance.

**Example: The Akali Dal demands an independent Sikh state in the Punjab, or at minimum, autonomy and guaranteed representation far exceeding their 2% population share.**

Their *position* sounds maximalist. But the *interest* beneath it is existential: the Punjab is the most dangerous flashpoint of any partition. Sikh farmers built the canal colonies that made it India's breadbasket. Amritsar, the holiest Sikh city, sits in the middle of a region claimed by Muslims, Hindus, and Sikhs alike. If India is partitioned, the Punjab must be divided, and no line can be drawn without leaving millions of Sikhs on the wrong side. The Akali Dal isn't demanding a state for its own sake -- they're demanding survival. They fear being swallowed by whichever larger nation absorbs the Punjab.

**Try saying:** "Sardar Baldev Singh, Master Tara Singh -- you've made clear that the Sikh community cannot accept an outcome that leaves Amritsar, the canal colonies, and Sikh farmland under a government that doesn't represent Sikh interests. That's a serious concern that everyone at this table should take seriously. Let me put it to the room as a problem: How can any settlement -- united or partitioned -- guarantee the physical security, economic livelihood, and religious freedom of the Sikh community in the Punjab? I'd like to hear proposals from every faction."

You haven't endorsed Khalistan. But you've acknowledged the legitimate problem -- that any border through the Punjab threatens the Sikhs -- and forced every faction to respond. Congress must explain how a united India protects Sikhs. The League must explain what Pakistan offers them. The Sikhs get to evaluate concrete proposals instead of shouting into the void.

## The Pattern

In every case, the move is the same:

1. **Hear the position.** Don't dismiss it or argue with it.
2. **Ask what's underneath.** Why this demand? What problem does it solve? What fear does it address?
3. **Restate what you heard.** "So what matters to you is..."
4. **Look for overlap.** Where do different factions' interests converge?
5. **Pose the interest as a shared problem.** "How might we address X in a way that works for everyone in this room?"

You won't always succeed. Some interests at Simla may genuinely be incompatible -- Jinnah's demand for Muslim sovereignty and Nehru's demand for a strong central government may not have a middle ground that satisfies both. But more often than you'd expect, the hardest-sounding demands turn out to be one particular answer to a question that has other answers too. Your job is to find the question.

## One More Thing

Your role sheet tells you to use your silence strategically. This advice works the same way. When two factions are locked in a positional argument -- partition vs. unity, separate vs. joint electorates -- don't jump in with your own position. Ask a question instead. Let them answer. Then restate what you heard in terms of interests rather than demands. You'll be surprised how often the room shifts when someone finally names what everyone actually needs.
