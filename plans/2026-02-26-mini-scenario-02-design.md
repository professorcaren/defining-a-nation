# Mini-Scenario 02: The Swadeshi Crisis — Implementation Plan

> **For Claude:** REQUIRED SUB-SKILL: Use superpowers:executing-plans to implement this plan task-by-task.

**Goal:** Build complete game materials for Mini-Scenario 02, set during the 1905 Bengal Partition crisis, using a simultaneous envoy-negotiation format with secret vulnerabilities, active British manipulation, and religious symbolism as a concrete negotiation mechanic.

**Architecture:** Six documents: handout, role cards, instructor guide, movement platform worksheet, faction tools (British tools + Indian action cards), and a secretariat guide for the Congress Moderates. All materials in `Mini-scenario_02-1905/`. No chair script — but the Moderates serve as secretariat, collecting coalition agreements (replacing the chair role from Scenario 01).

**Key mechanic — two agreement channels:**
- **Coalition agreements** (deals between Indian factions) are submitted to the **Congress Moderates' table**, who serve as secretariat. The Moderates see every coalition deal.
- **British side deals** (offers from the British to individual factions) go **directly to the receiving faction** and are NOT submitted to the Moderates. Congress doesn't know these deals exist until the final reveal.
- At the reveal, the instructor collects ALL agreements from both channels and reads them aloud — this is when the Moderates discover how much happened behind their back.

**Format conventions:** Match Scenario 01's markdown style. Role cards use the same section structure (position, leverage, what you can offer, red line, private information, opening move) but replace MAP/Best Deal boxes with Vulnerability sections and drop the scaffolding as specified in the Mini-scenarios plan.

## Task 1: Create directory and write `handout.md`

**Files:**
- Create: `Mini-scenario_02-1905/handout.md`

**Content spec:**

The student-facing handout. One page when printed. Sections:

1. **Title:** "The Swadeshi Crisis: Bengal, 1905"
2. **The Situation** (~150 words): Lord Curzon has partitioned Bengal. East Bengal is Muslim-majority; West Bengal is Hindu-majority. The Swadeshi boycott is erupting. But the movement is fracturing before it starts. Representatives of five factions have gathered to decide whether they can build a coalition broad enough to force the British to reverse the partition.
3. **The Central Question:** "Can the anti-partition movement build a coalition broad enough to force the British to reverse the partition — and what will that coalition cost?"
4. **The Factions table:** 5 rows (Congress Moderates, Congress Extremists, Muslim Leaders of East Bengal, Bengali Hindu Zamindars, British Administration). Columns: Faction | Who They Are | What They Bring.
5. **How This Works** — the envoy rules:
   - Each faction sits at a table. You strategize together.
   - Each round, your faction sends **2 envoys** to other tables to negotiate. The rest stay home and strategize.
   - **Coalition agreements** (deals between Indian factions) must be written on an index card and submitted to the **Congress Moderates' table**. The Moderates serve as secretariat — they collect, record, and hold all coalition agreements. The agreement must include: which factions are joining, what each commits to do, and which items from the Movement Platform (separate sheet) the coalition adopts.
   - **Deals with the British** work differently. If you accept a British offer, you keep the card. You do NOT submit it to the Moderates. These deals are private between you and the British.
   - You may sign agreements with multiple factions. You may sign contradictory agreements. But if another faction discovers you've made incompatible promises, expect consequences.
   - Every faction has a **special tool** — an action card you can play once during the game. Check your role card for details.
   - The British faction has additional special tools. They will use them.
6. **Rules** — same four rules as Scenario 01 (argue from role, reveal/conceal, side deals allowed, no right answer).

**Style:** Match the tone and formatting of `Mini-scenario_01-1857/handout.md` exactly.

## Task 3: Write `british-tools.md`

**Files:**
- Create: `Mini-scenario_02-1905/british-tools.md`

**Content spec:**

Printable cards for the British faction only. Brief header explaining the three tools. Then the individual cards, formatted for cutting.

**Header (for British eyes only):**
"You have three special tools. Use them at any time during any round. You do not need permission. Simply send an envoy with the relevant card or slip."

**Tool 1: The Leak** (use once per round)
You may reveal another faction's secret vulnerability to any table. Send an envoy to that table and read the leak aloud. Three pre-written leak cards:

- **Leak: Congress Moderates.** "You should know that two of the Congress Moderates' leaders have already accepted seats on the Viceroy's legislative council. They are protesting a system they are participating in. Ask them about it."
- **Leak: Muslim Leaders.** "You should know that three leading members of the Muslim delegation hold paid positions in the new East Bengal provincial government created by the partition. Their opposition to reversal is personal, not just communal."
- **Leak: Bengali Zamindars.** "You should know that several of the leading zamindars are deeply indebted to banks controlled by the British government. Their debts can be called in at any time. Their independence is not what it appears."

**Tool 2: The Private Offer** (use any time)
Pre-written offer cards to hand to specific factions. The receiving faction keeps the card. They may show it to others or keep it secret.

- **To Muslim Leaders:** "His Majesty's Government proposes: separate electorates for Muslims in any future legislative council. Your community will elect its own representatives. You will never be outvoted by the Hindu majority. This guarantee stands regardless of whether Bengal is reunified. All we ask: do not join the boycott."
- **To Bengali Zamindars:** "His Majesty's Government proposes: your debts to government-controlled banks will be forgiven. Your estates will be guaranteed in perpetuity under the Permanent Settlement. All we ask: do not fund the Swadeshi movement."
- **To Congress Moderates:** "His Majesty's Government proposes: two additional Indian seats on the Viceroy's legislative council, plus a formal review of the Bengal partition within five years. All we ask: publicly condemn the Extremists' violence and distance yourself from their methods."

**Tool 3: The Rumor** (use any time)
Write any claim on a slip of paper and have an envoy deliver it to any table. It does not have to be true. Examples (but the British faction may invent their own):
- "We have heard that the Extremists plan to extend the boycott to all Muslim-owned businesses, not just those selling foreign cloth."
- "We understand the Zamindars are negotiating privately with the Moderates to fund a constitutional petition instead of supporting the boycott."
- "A senior Muslim leader has told us privately that he would support the partition being reversed — if Congress drops Vande Mataram."

**Step 1:** Write british-tools.md.
**Step 2:** Verify each leak matches the vulnerability on the corresponding role card.
**Step 3:** Commit.

## Task 4: Write `role-cards.md`

**Files:**
- Create: `Mini-scenario_02-1905/role-cards.md`

**Content spec:**

Five faction role cards. Each card follows this structure (adapted from Scenario 01):
- **Your position** — what the faction publicly wants
- **Your leverage** — bargaining power
- **What you can offer** — concrete tradeables
- **Your red line** — what they won't accept
- **Your vulnerability** — NEW: the secret weakness (replaces MAP/Best Deal box per the plan's scaffolding reduction)
- **Your private information** — something to reveal or conceal
- **Your opening move** — first action suggestion
- **Envoy assignments** — instructions for assigning 2 envoys per round

No MAP/Best Deal box (dropped per the Mini-scenarios plan: "Consider dropping it in Scenarios 2 and 3 to increase difficulty").

### Card 1: Congress Moderates (Gokhale's Circle)

- **Position:** Reverse the partition through constitutional petition and pressure on sympathetic British MPs. The boycott is dangerous — it will provoke repression and alienate moderates in London.
- **Leverage:** Connections to sympathetic British liberals (Wedderburn, Hume's allies). Credibility with the Viceroy's office. The only faction the British will negotiate with seriously.
- **What you can offer:** Diplomatic legitimacy. A coalition with the Moderates can claim to be "reasonable" and "constitutional" — harder for the British to dismiss. Access to sympathetic British press coverage.
- **Red line:** No violence. No Hindu religious imagery that makes the movement look sectarian. The movement must remain secular and constitutional or you walk.
- **Vulnerability:** Two of your leaders have already accepted seats on the Viceroy's legislative council. You are protesting a system you participate in. If this becomes public, your credibility as opponents of British policy collapses. The British know this.
- **Private information:** You have received word that a group of sympathetic British MPs will introduce a motion in Parliament questioning the partition — but only if the movement in India remains nonviolent and avoids Hindu sectarian imagery. If the movement adopts Vande Mataram and cow protection, the MPs will withdraw their support. You can use this to argue against Hindu symbols, but revealing it also reveals how dependent you are on British goodwill.
- **Opening move:** Send envoys to the Zamindars (you need their money) and to the Muslim Leaders (you need their participation to prove the movement is national, not Hindu). Offer: a purely secular, constitutional movement with no Hindu imagery. Your pitch: "This is the only version of the movement that can actually win."
- **Your special role — Secretariat:** Your faction collects and holds all coalition agreements between Indian factions. When any two factions sign a deal, they submit the index card to your table. This means you see every coalition deal — who's allied with whom, what they've promised, which platform items they've adopted. Assign one member to manage incoming agreements. **Important: deals made directly with the British are NOT submitted to you.** You won't know about those until the final reveal.
- **Your action card — "Appeal to London":** Play once during the game. Announce to the room: "Our allies in Parliament have agreed to introduce a motion questioning the partition — but ONLY if the movement remains nonviolent and avoids sectarian imagery. Any coalition that includes [name specific platform items] will lose Parliamentary support." This forces other factions to weigh your London connection against the Extremists' mobilization tools.
- **Envoy assignments:** Assign 2 members as envoys each round. One member stays at the table as **secretariat** — receiving agreements and visiting envoys. Remaining members strategize.

### Card 2: Congress Extremists (Tilak's Followers)

- **Position:** Full Swadeshi boycott. Burn British cloth. Shut down British shops. Use every tool available to mobilize the masses — including Hindu festivals, Vande Mataram, and appeals to Mother India. The Moderates' petitions have achieved nothing in 20 years. The streets are where power lives.
- **Leverage:** Street-level mobilization. You have student networks, newspaper editors, and organizers who can fill public squares with thousands of people in 48 hours. No other faction can do this. Without you, the movement has leaders but no followers.
- **What you can offer:** Mass mobilization. A boycott that actually bites. The energy that turns a petition into a movement. But your mobilization machine runs on Hindu cultural fuel — that's what moves people in the streets.
- **Red line:** You will not strip the movement of its soul to make it palatable to people who may not join anyway. Vande Mataram stays. If the movement becomes a polite petition with no emotional power, you're out.
- **Vulnerability:** Your student networks in Barisal have already beaten Muslim shopkeepers who were selling British cloth. You cannot fully control your own people. If this becomes public before you've secured a coalition, every other faction will point to it as proof that your movement is anti-Muslim, not anti-British. You don't know yet that the British know about this. (They will learn in the mid-game intervention.)
- **Private information:** You know that the Moderates' strategy of petitioning Parliament is a dead end. You have seen a leaked copy of Home Secretary Risley's secret memo: "Bengal united is a power. Bengal divided will pull in different ways... One of our main objects is to split up and thereby to weaken a solid body of opponents to our rule." The partition was never about administrative efficiency. It was a deliberate political attack. You can share this memo to radicalize wavering factions — but it may also frighten the Zamindars into thinking the British are too powerful to oppose.
- **Opening move:** Send envoys to the Zamindars (you need their money to fund the boycott) and to the Moderates (you need their respectability, even if you despise their methods). Your pitch: "The boycott is already working — textile imports are dropping. Join us or be irrelevant."
- **Your action card — "Call a Hartal":** Play once during the game. Announce to the room: "A general strike has been called across Calcutta. All shops will close. All British goods will be refused. Every faction must now state publicly: do you support the hartal or do you cross the picket line?" This forces every faction to declare a side — there is no abstaining from a hartal. Factions that cross the line are publicly marked as opponents of the movement.
- **Envoy assignments:** Assign 2 members as envoys each round. Remaining members strategize at home and receive visiting envoys.

### Card 3: Muslim Leaders of East Bengal

- **Position:** The partition benefits Muslims. For the first time, East Bengal's Muslim majority has its own province, its own government, its own institutions not dominated by Hindu elites. You are open to cooperation with the anti-partition movement, but only if it serves Muslim interests. If the movement is just Hindus fighting to restore Hindu dominance over Bengal, you have no reason to join.
- **Leverage:** Legitimacy. Without Muslim participation, the anti-partition movement is a Hindu movement, and the British can dismiss it as sectarian. Every other faction needs you to prove the movement is "national." That gives you enormous bargaining power.
- **What you can offer:** The appearance of Hindu-Muslim unity — the one thing the British fear most and the one thing the movement needs most to succeed. Your participation transforms the coalition from a Hindu grievance into an Indian cause.
- **Red line:** No Hindu religious imagery in the movement. No Vande Mataram (a hymn about Hindus defeating Muslims). No cow protection (an attack on Muslim practice). No Kali banners. If the movement looks Hindu, sounds Hindu, and feels Hindu, it is Hindu — and you are out.
- **Vulnerability:** Three of your leading members hold paid positions in the new East Bengal provincial government. You benefit personally from the partition, not just communally. If this becomes public, your principled stance on Muslim interests looks like self-interest. The British know this.
- **Private information:** The Nawab of Dhaka has received a loan from the British government to support Muslim institutions in East Bengal. You are not as independent of British support as you appear. But you also know that the British are using you — Risley's memo (which you have not seen) makes clear the partition was designed to divide Indians, not to help Muslims. You suspect this but cannot prove it. If another faction shares evidence of British manipulation, it might change your calculus.
- **Opening move:** Send envoys to the Moderates (the faction most likely to offer a secular movement) and to the Extremists (to find out what they're actually planning). Your pitch to both: "We will join — but only if the movement drops every Hindu symbol. What are you willing to give up?"
- **Your action card — "Publish an Open Letter":** Play once during the game. Write a brief public statement (2-3 sentences) on a slip of paper and hand it to the instructor, who reads it aloud to the room. This is your conditions for joining the movement, stated on the record. Once published, every faction must respond to your terms publicly. Use this when you want to force the coalition to commit — or when you want to expose that they can't.
- **Envoy assignments:** Assign 2 members as envoys each round. Remaining members strategize and receive visiting envoys.

### Card 4: Bengali Hindu Zamindars

- **Position:** Reverse the partition. Your estates straddle the partition line — the new border splits your holdings between two provinces with different tax systems, different courts, and different administrators. This is an economic disaster for you. But you also fear mass mobilization. Street protests and boycotts are unpredictable. A movement that empowers peasants and students today may turn on landlords tomorrow.
- **Leverage:** Money. The boycott needs funding: printed pamphlets, organized rallies, alternative Indian-made goods to replace British imports. You can bankroll the movement — or starve it.
- **What you can offer:** Financial support for the Swadeshi movement. Funding for Indian textile production to replace boycotted British goods. Institutional respectability — zamindars joining the movement signals this is not just a student protest.
- **Red line:** No land reform. No rhetoric about "the people" that implies redistribution. The movement fights the British, not the landlord class.
- **Vulnerability:** Several of you are deeply indebted to banks controlled by the British government. If the British call in those loans, you lose everything. Your ability to fund the movement — or even to join it — depends on the British choosing not to destroy you financially. The British know this.
- **Private information:** You have heard that the Extremists' student organizers in Barisal are enforcing the boycott with threats and beatings. Some of the victims are Muslim traders. You can use this information to discredit the Extremists and push the movement toward Moderate leadership — or keep it quiet to avoid fracturing the coalition before it forms.
- **Opening move:** Send envoys to the Moderates (your natural allies — constitutional, respectable, non-threatening) and to the Extremists (you need to know what they're planning, and they need your money). Your pitch: "We'll fund the movement — but we need guarantees about what kind of movement it will be."
- **Your action card — "Fund or Defund":** Play once during the game. Announce publicly that you are either **funding** a specific faction's activities or **withdrawing funding** from a faction you previously supported. Name the faction. This makes your financial leverage visible and forces the funded faction to acknowledge their dependence on you — or forces a defunded faction to scramble for resources. (If you fund someone, hand them an index card marked "FUNDED BY ZAMINDARS" — they may show it to others as proof of support, or hide it.)
- **Envoy assignments:** Assign 2 members as envoys each round. Remaining members strategize and receive visiting envoys.

### Card 5: The British Administration

- **Position:** Maintain the partition. Keep order. Prevent a united front. Your job is not to crush the movement — that would create martyrs. Your job is to ensure the movement never unifies. As long as the factions are divided, you win.
- **Leverage:** Power. You govern Bengal. You control the courts, the police, the banks, and the civil service. You can make offers no other faction can match. And you have intelligence about every faction's weaknesses.
- **What you can offer:** Concrete deals: separate electorates for Muslims, debt forgiveness for zamindars, council seats for moderates. You can also offer information — you know secrets about other factions that they don't know you know.
- **Red line:** The partition stands. You will negotiate everything else, but Bengal stays divided. If a unified movement forms that genuinely threatens the partition, escalate to repression — but only as a last resort.
- **Vulnerability:** You have no military reinforcements available. Troops are committed to the Northwest Frontier and cannot be redeployed to Bengal. If a truly unified mass movement emerges — Hindus and Muslims together, streets and courts together — you cannot suppress it by force. You are bluffing strength. NO OTHER FACTION KNOWS THIS. Protect this secret above all else.
- **Private information:** You have Home Secretary Risley's secret memo confirming that the partition was designed to divide Indians: "Bengal united is a power. Bengal divided will pull in different ways." If this document reaches the Indian factions, it could radicalize moderates and unify the movement. The Extremists have a copy (see their card), but they may not know you know they have it.
- **Your special tools:** You have three tools no other faction has. See the separate "British Tools" sheet for details. In brief:
  - **The Leak:** Once per round, reveal another faction's vulnerability to any table.
  - **The Private Offer:** Hand a pre-written deal to any faction at any time.
  - **The Rumor:** Write any claim (true or false) on a slip and deliver it to any table.
- **Your strategy:** You have six envoy slots across three rounds. Use them aggressively. Don't wait for factions to come to you. Go to them. Your goal is not to be liked — it's to ensure that no coalition forms that includes both Hindus and Muslims. The Movement Platform is your best weapon: every Hindu symbol the coalition includes drives Muslims away. Encourage the Extremists to insist on their symbols. Offer the Muslims a better deal than the coalition can.
- **Opening move:** Round 1: Send envoys to the Muslim Leaders (offer separate electorates) and to the Zamindars (remind them about their debts, offer forgiveness). Save your leaks for Round 2, after coalitions have started forming — a well-timed leak does more damage to an existing alliance than to factions still shopping.

## Task 5: Write `instructor-guide.md`

**Files:**
- Create: `Mini-scenario_02-1905/instructor-guide.md`

**Content spec:**

Comprehensive instructor guide matching the depth and format of `Mini-scenario_01-1857/instructor-guide.md`. Sections:

### Overview table
| | |
|---|---|
| **Paired with** | Reading 2: The Birth of Indian Nationalism |
| **Time** | 75 minutes |
| **Students** | 5 factions; works with 20–35 students (~6 per table) |
| **Materials** | Handout (shared), movement platform worksheet (shared), 5 role cards (confidential), British tools sheet (British faction only), index cards + slips of paper for agreements/rumors |
| **Negotiation concept** | Coalition-building with misaligned allies |

### Learning Objectives
1. Explain why the Swadeshi movement succeeded as a Hindu mobilization but failed as a national movement
2. Identify how religious symbolism can simultaneously strengthen and fracture a coalition
3. Recognize active manipulation as a strategy distinct from direct repression (the British win not by force but by offering better deals)

### Card Structure
Describe the new structure (vulnerability replaces MAP/Best Deal, no scaffolding).

### Before Class
- Print handout (one per student or on screen)
- Print movement platform worksheet (one per table)
- Print role cards (cut, one per faction)
- Print British tools (one copy, British faction only)
- Bring index cards (for coalition agreements) and blank slips (for rumors)
- Assign students to factions (~6 per table for 32 students; see Running with 30+ below)
- Arrange room: 5 table clusters with space for movement between them

### Setup (10 minutes)
1. Frame the game (2 min): "You've read about the birth of Indian nationalism. Today you're in 1905. The British have split Bengal. A boycott movement is erupting. Your job is to build — or prevent — a coalition. But this is not a debate. There are no speeches. You negotiate by sending envoys to other tables and signing written agreements."
2. Distribute materials (3 min): Handout + movement platform to everyone. Role cards to each faction (confidential). British tools to British faction only. Index cards to each table. Blank slips to British table.
3. Explain the two agreement channels (2 min): "There are two kinds of deals in this game. **Coalition agreements** between Indian factions go to the Congress Moderates' table — they are the secretariat and they collect all coalition deals. **Deals with the British** are different — those stay private between you and the British. The Moderates don't see them. At the end, we reveal everything." Also: "Every faction has one action card on your role sheet — a special move you can play once during the game. Read it carefully."
4. Faction prep (3 min): Read cards, assign envoys for Round 1, assign one Moderate as secretariat, discuss strategy.

### Round 1: "Can We Unite?" (15 minutes)
- Envoys fan out. Factions strategize internally and receive visitors.
- The British should be sending envoys immediately — don't let them wait.
- Watch for: Who approaches the Muslim Leaders first? What platform items come up? Do the Extremists lead with symbols or try to suppress them?
- At 15 min, call time. Collect any signed agreements. Read aloud which factions have signed agreements (not the terms — just who's allied with whom). This creates public information for Round 2.

### Mid-Game Intervention (between Rounds 1 and 2)
Read aloud:
> "News from Barisal. Swadeshi volunteers have burned a shipment of Lancashire cloth outside a Muslim merchant's shop. The merchant was beaten. His stock was destroyed. The volunteers were chanting Vande Mataram. The merchant's family is Hindu — his wife is from a Hindu village. The volunteers did not ask. They saw a Muslim name on the shop."

**What this does:** Confirms the Extremists' vulnerability (they can't control their people). Forces every faction to recalculate. The Muslim Leaders now have evidence — not rumor — that the movement targets Muslims. The Moderates can say "we warned you." The Zamindars who knew about the violence must decide whether to reveal that they knew. The British can use this to drive wedges.

**Instructor note:** The Zamindars' role card gives them private knowledge of the Barisal violence. Watch whether they revealed it before the intervention. If they did, the intervention confirms their intelligence. If they didn't, other factions may ask: "Did you know about this?"

### Round 2: "At What Cost?" (15 minutes)
- The coalition question shifts. The violence is public.
- The British should now deploy their leaks (Muslim Leaders' paid positions, Zamindars' debts) to maximum effect.
- British offer cards should be in play — especially the separate electorates offer to Muslim Leaders.
- Watch for: Does anyone try to amend the movement platform to drop symbols? Do the Extremists dig in or compromise? Are the Muslim Leaders still at the table?
- At 15 min, call time. Collect agreements. Read aloud.

### Round 3: "The Fracture" (10 minutes, compressed)
Instructor announces:
> "The Viceroy has sent a private message to the Muslim delegation. He proposes: separate electorates for Muslims in any future legislative council. Muslim voters would elect Muslim representatives. No Hindu majority could ever outvote you. This offer stands regardless of what happens to the partition."

**What this does:** This is the 1906 Simla Deputation offer — the foundational moment of Muslim separatism. It gives the Muslim Leaders a concrete alternative to the coalition. They don't need the Swadeshi movement if the British will protect their political interests directly.

- 10 minutes of final negotiation. All envoys out.
- At 10 min, call time. Collect all final agreements.

### The Reveal (5 minutes)
First, ask the Moderates' secretariat to read aloud all coalition agreements they received. Let the room hear what the Moderates think happened.

Then collect all British side deals from the factions that accepted them. Read these aloud one by one. Watch the Moderates' faces. This is the moment they discover how much was happening outside their view.

Note:
- Which factions allied? On what terms?
- Which movement platform items were included?
- Did any faction sign contradictory agreements — a coalition deal submitted to the Moderates AND a British side deal kept secret?
- Did the Muslim Leaders take the British deal? When?
- Did the British bluff hold? (Did anyone discover they had no military reinforcements?)
- How many deals did the Moderates NOT know about?

### Debrief (15 minutes)

**Step 1: What Happened (3 min)**
Ask a student from each faction to state their final position in one sentence. Then ask: "How many of the five platform symbols ended up in your coalition's agreements?"

**Step 2: Name the Concept (5 min)**
Introduce **coalition-building with misaligned allies**:
> "Every faction in this room opposed the partition — except the British and (possibly) the Muslim Leaders. You shared a position. But building a coalition required agreeing on *how* to fight, and that's where it fell apart. The Extremists needed Hindu symbols to mobilize. The Muslim Leaders couldn't join a movement that used them. The Moderates needed respectability. The Zamindars needed safety. You were misaligned allies: you agreed on the goal but couldn't agree on the method, the symbols, or the cost."

Ask: "Can anyone give me an example where two factions wanted the same thing but couldn't work together because of *how* they wanted to get it?"

Ask: "The British had three tools: leaks, offers, and rumors. Which one did the most damage? Why?"

Ask the Moderates: "You were the secretariat. You saw every coalition deal. How much of the game did you think you controlled? How much did you actually control?" Then ask the other Indian factions: "How many of you accepted a British deal that the Moderates never saw?" This is the core lesson about information and power: the faction that thinks it sees everything is the easiest to blindside.

**Step 3: Connect to the Reading (5 min)**
Reading 2: "The militant nationalists' sincerity about Hindu-Muslim unity did not prevent their methods from undermining it."

Ask: "Did your game replicate that? Were the Extremists sincere about including Muslims? Did their methods undermine it anyway?"

Reading 2: "Communalism... was 'political trade in religion.' Its roots were economic as much as religious."

Ask: "Was the fracture in your game about religion or about interests? Did the Muslim Leaders leave because of Vande Mataram or because the British offered a better deal?"

Reading 2: Risley's memo — "Bengal united is a power. Bengal divided will pull in different ways."

Ask the British faction: "Did you create the divisions, or did you exploit divisions that already existed?"

**Step 4: Look Ahead (2 min)**
> "The Swadeshi movement historically succeeded in mobilizing Hindus but failed to become a national movement. The Muslim League was founded in 1906, one year after the partition, and demanded separate electorates — exactly what the British offered in your game. Next class, we'll read about Gandhi, who tried to solve this problem by building a movement that transcended religious identity. Did it work? That's Reading 3."

### What to Watch For (detailed)
- **Congress Moderates paralyzed by their vulnerability.** If the British leak their council seats early, the Moderates may spend the whole game defending their credibility instead of building a coalition. That's realistic but watch that they don't disengage entirely. If they stall, whisper: "Your MPs in London are waiting. What coalition can you present to them?"
- **Extremists insisting on all five symbols.** This is the most common pattern and the most historically accurate. If they won't compromise on any symbol, the Muslim Leaders walk and the Moderates may too. Let it play out — this IS the lesson. But if the Extremists seem confused about why symbols matter, whisper: "The Muslim Leaders told you their condition. Are you going to meet it?"
- **Muslim Leaders taking the British deal in Round 2.** If the separate electorates offer comes early and the movement platform is full of Hindu symbols, rational Muslim Leaders will take the deal. This is historically what happened. Don't prevent it — debrief it. "At what point did you decide the coalition wasn't worth it?"
- **Zamindars frozen by debt.** The British can neutralize the Zamindars with a single offer card. Watch whether the Zamindars tell other factions about the British pressure or hide it. If they disengage, whisper: "The partition split your estates. The British offer protects your debts but doesn't fix that. What would?"
- **British faction sitting passively.** The most common failure mode. The British should be the busiest table in the room. If they're not sending envoys, walk over and say: "You have three tools and six envoy slots across three rounds. Use them. Who are you targeting first?"
- **A coalition actually forming with Muslim support.** This is rare but possible if the Extremists compromise on symbols and the Moderates broker a secular platform. If it happens, celebrate it in debrief — and then ask: "The Reading says this kind of unity lasted from 1916 to 1922. Six years. Why didn't it last?"

### Timing Adjustments
**For 60 min:** Cut to 2 rounds (drop Round 3). Deliver the separate electorates offer as part of the mid-game intervention instead. Reduce debrief to 10 min.

**For 90 min:** Extend each round by 3-5 min. Add a "faction caucus" at the end of each round where factions regroup, share intelligence from envoys, and plan the next round.

### Running with 30+ Students
With 32 students: ~6 per table. The envoy format naturally keeps everyone active:
- 2 envoys out per round = 2 students physically at other tables negotiating
- Remaining 4 at home = receiving envoys from other factions, strategizing, drafting agreements
- Rotate envoy assignments each round so different students go out

If you have 35+, add a 6th faction: **Bengali Student Activists** — young, radical, uncontrollable, the foot soldiers of the Swadeshi boycott. They are the ones doing the Barisal beatings. Give them high mobilization power but no political legitimacy. They need the Extremists for direction and the Moderates for cover.

### Common Problems and Fixes
| Problem | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Students don't understand the envoy mechanic | Walk through it physically: "You two, stand up, walk to that table, ask them what they want. Report back in 3 minutes." |
| Nobody signs written agreements | After Round 1, announce: "Zero agreements have been signed. You have two rounds left. If no coalition exists by the end, the British win by default." |
| British faction doesn't use their tools | Walk to the British table and say: "Who are you leaking to? Who are you making an offer to? Pick one of each and send envoys now." |
| One faction dominates all negotiations | In the next round, give them a setback: "Rumors are spreading that [dominant faction] has been making promises it can't keep. Other factions are wary." |
| Muslim Leaders leave the game early | This is historically accurate — but keep them engaged. Say: "You've taken the British deal. But the deal isn't signed yet. What else can you extract from the British while they still need you?" |
| Agreements are vague ("we agree to cooperate") | Reject them. "This agreement doesn't specify which platform items you adopt. Rewrite it with specifics." |
| Students argue from hindsight | Redirect: "You don't know yet that the Muslim League will be founded next year. What do you know right now, in 1905?" |

### Notes for Later in the Course
- The separate electorates offer in this scenario is the same institutional mechanism that structures the entire Simla conference in the main game. Students who experienced it here understand its power viscerally.
- The Movement Platform mechanic (negotiating over symbols) previews the constitutional negotiations at Simla, where factions must agree on institutional design, not just goals.
- Students who played the British will understand "divide and rule" not as a slogan but as a set of specific tools. This helps when they read about the Cripps Mission and the Cabinet Mission in the main game.
- The Moderates-vs-Extremists split in this scenario maps onto the Congress moderates-vs-hawks dynamic at Simla.
- The envoy format itself is preparation for the main game, where simultaneous caucusing and back-channel dealing drive the action.
- The Moderates' secretariat experience — thinking they saw everything while British side deals happened in the dark — directly previews Congress's position at Simla. Congress believed it represented all of India; the League had been building a separate relationship with the British for decades. Students who played the Moderates will viscerally understand Congress's blindspot in the main game.
