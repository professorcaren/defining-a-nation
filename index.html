Defining a Nation: Readings

Primary sources from Rachel Fell McDermott et al., eds., Sources of Indian Tradition: Modern India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh, 3rd ed. (Columbia University Press, 2014). All students read the preparatory reading and its paired primary sources before class. Faction-specific readings are assigned only to members of that faction. Some readings appear under multiple factions.

Before You Begin

Scenario Book: Defining a Nation
India on the Eve of Independence, 1945

Read the full scenario book before anything else. It introduces the Simla conference, the factions, the rules, and the central question you will be debating.

Part 1: What Is India?

Preparatory Reading: What Is India?
From the Mughals through the British Conquest

Primary Sources (all students)

  • Rammohan Roy: The Future of India (1 p.)
    The earliest major Indian intellectual asks what India should become under British rule.
  • Shah Abdul-Aziz: Islam in Danger (2 pp.)
    A Muslim scholar declares British-ruled India is no longer safe for Islam. The seed of the two-nation question.
  • Queen Victoria: Proclamation of 1858 (3 pp.)
    The constitutional framework establishing the British Raj. Every faction at Simla is arguing about dissolving this document.

Interactive

The Last Maharaja: Survival 1790–1857
A decision game about sovereignty under the East India Company

Play through 67 years as the ruler of a princely state, balancing your treasury, British favor, and your people's loyalty. Every choice the game presents mirrors a real dilemma faced by Indian rulers—and illustrates why most lost their thrones before a shot was fired.

Part 2: The Birth of Indian Nationalism

Preparatory Reading: The Birth of Indian Nationalism
From the Indian National Congress to Gandhi's Arrival (1885–1919)

Primary Sources (all students)

  • Dadabhai Naoroji: The Pros and Cons of British Rule (4 pp.)
    The "drain of wealth" argument: Britain is impoverishing India. Congress still uses this case at Simla.
  • Bankim Chandra Chatterji: Hail to the Mother (Vande Mataram) (7 pp.)
    The poem that became the anthem of Indian nationalism. Its Hindu imagery explains why it alienated Muslims.
  • Sayyid Ahmad Khan: The Indian National Congress as a Danger and a Folly (8 pp.)
    The founding argument for Muslim separatism. Khan warns that democracy means Hindu domination.

Interactive

Divide and Rule: The Bengal Partition Crisis of 1905
A strategy game about colonial administration and Indian resistance

Play as the Viceroy's private secretary managing the Bengal Partition crisis. Balance legitimacy, stability, and the swadeshi boycott movement across three years of decisions—and discover why "divide and rule" carried costs the British didn't anticipate.

Part 3: Gandhi, Ambedkar, and the Fracturing

Preparatory Reading: Gandhi, Ambedkar, and the Fracturing
From Non-Cooperation to the Eve of Simla (1920–1945)

Primary Sources (all students)

  • Ambedkar: The Evils of Caste (5 pp.)
    Ambedkar's indictment of the caste system, delivered as a speech to a caste Hindu audience.
  • Gandhi: Responses to Ambedkar's Indictment (3 pp.)
    Gandhi's reply. Read alongside Ambedkar to understand the Poona Pact and the stakes of separate electorates.
  • Muhammad Ali Jinnah: Founder of Pakistan (9 pp.)
    Jinnah's case for Pakistan, including the 1940 Lahore Resolution. The central argument you will confront at Simla.

Faction-Specific Readings

The following readings are assigned to members of specific factions. Some readings appear under multiple factions—if a reading is listed for your faction, you should read it regardless of whether another faction also has it assigned.

British

You preside over the conference. Understand the ideology behind British rule and the frameworks that have been proposed.

  • Thomas Babington Macaulay: The Minute on Education (3 pp.)
    The civilizing-mission ideology that shaped British India's education system and administration.
  • The Cabinet Mission Plan: The Last British Offer (6 pp.)
    The British framework for transferring power. The kind of proposal you will need to draft at Simla.
  • Congress's Response to the Cabinet Mission Plan (2 pp.)
    How Congress reacted to the British proposal. Anticipate these objections when crafting your own.
Indian National Congress (INC)

You claim to speak for all Indians. These readings show both the promise and the vulnerability of that claim.

  • The Lucknow Pact, 1916 (6 pp.)
    Congress's model for Hindu-Muslim unity: the agreement on reserved seats that briefly held the movement together.
  • Sarojini Naidu: For the Evolution of National Life (4 pp.)
    Congress secularism articulated by one of its most prominent voices.
  • Jawaharlal Nehru: "Be Not Afraid" (5 pp.)
    Nehru's socialist, modernizing vision for India's future.
  • Abul Kalam Azad: The Muslims of India and the Future of India (8 pp.)
    A Muslim leader who opposes partition and stays with Congress. Your strongest evidence that Congress speaks for Muslims too. Also assigned to: Muslim League
  • The Rajagopalachari Formula and Gandhi-Jinnah Correspondence (6 pp.)
    The failed 1944 negotiations. Study what went wrong to understand what you're up against at Simla.
  • Gandhi: Hindu-Muslim Tension, Its Cause and Cure (3 pp.)
    Your ally Gandhi's diagnosis of communal conflict. A framework for the unity argument. Also assigned to: Gandhi's Adherents
  • The Cabinet Mission Plan: The Last British Offer (6 pp.)
    The British framework for transferring power. Know what the British have proposed so you can shape their next offer. Also assigned to: British, Muslim League, Princely States
Muslim League

You demand Pakistan. These readings trace the intellectual case for a separate Muslim state—and the arguments you must overcome.

  • Muhammad Iqbal: A Separate Muslim State, and Letters to Jinnah (6 pp.)
    The intellectual foundation for Pakistan, from the poet-philosopher who inspired the idea.
  • Choudhary Rahmat Ali: "The Fatherland of the Pak Nation" (3 pp.)
    The man who coined the name "Pakistan" and mapped its geography.
  • Mohamed Ali: Hindu-Muslim Unity, Then Adversary of Gandhi (7 pp.)
    A Muslim leader who allied with Gandhi during non-cooperation, then turned against him. The trajectory from unity to separation.
  • Begum Shaista Ikramullah: Cherished Encounters with Jinnah (9 pp.)
    A Muslim League woman's account of Jinnah and the movement for Pakistan.
  • Abul Kalam Azad: The Muslims of India and the Future of India (8 pp.)
    A Muslim leader who opposes partition. Know this argument—Congress will use Azad against you. Also assigned to: Congress
  • B. R. Ambedkar: Must There Be a Pakistan? (8 pp.)
    A non-Muslim's strategic analysis of partition. Ambedkar takes the demand for Pakistan seriously—and probes its weaknesses. Also assigned to: SCF
  • The Cabinet Mission Plan: The Last British Offer (6 pp.)
    The British framework for transferring power. The provisions on grouping provinces are especially relevant to Pakistan. Also assigned to: British, Congress, Princely States
Gandhi's Adherents

You carry Gandhi's moral authority. Master his arguments—and know the strongest objections to them.

  • Gandhi: Hind Swaraj (6 pp.)
    Gandhi's foundational text rejecting Western modernity. The philosophical basis for village self-rule and nonviolence.
  • Gandhi: The Crime of Chauri Chaura (3 pp.)
    Gandhi calls off the entire non-cooperation movement after a single act of violence. What nonviolence demands.
  • Gandhi: Hindu-Muslim Tension, Its Cause and Cure (3 pp.)
    Gandhi's diagnosis of communal conflict and his prescription for healing it. Directly relevant to Simla. Also assigned to: Congress
  • Gandhi: Letter to Lord Irwin (Salt Satyagraha) (5 pp.)
    Gandhi explains the Salt March to the Viceroy. Satyagraha in practice.
  • Gandhi: From the Gandhi-Irwin Pact to Quit India (3 pp.)
    The immediate political backdrop to Simla. The Quit India resolution and its consequences.
  • Gandhi: Untouchability and Swaraj (4 pp.)
    Gandhi on caste, untouchability, and women—the village-level injustices that no constitution can solve from above. Also assigned to: Congress, SCF, Village Leaders
  • Tagore's Critique of Gandhi, and Gandhi's Response (9 pp.)
    The sharpest internal debate about nonviolence and non-cooperation.
  • Periyar Responds to Gandhi on Caste (4 pp.)
    The most devastating critique of Gandhi's approach to untouchability. Know what the SCF will throw at you. Also assigned to: SCF
Scheduled Caste Federation

You fight for 70 million Untouchables. Arm yourself with the intellectual case for political power—and understand every faction's posture on caste.

  • Jotirao Phule: The Tyranny of a Brahman-Dominated History (5 pp.)
    The intellectual precursor to Ambedkar: a 19th-century radical's attack on Brahmin domination.
  • Periyar Responds to Gandhi on Caste (4 pp.)
    Another anti-caste voice: Periyar's sharp critique of Gandhi's paternalism toward Untouchables. Also assigned to: Gandhi's Adherents
  • B. R. Ambedkar: Must There Be a Pakistan? (8 pp.)
    Ambedkar's strategic analysis of partition from an Untouchable perspective. Essential for your negotiations. Also assigned to: Muslim League
  • Gandhi: Untouchability and Swaraj (4 pp.)
    Gandhi's position on untouchability and its relationship to self-rule. Know the argument you are fighting against. Also assigned to: Congress, Gandhi's Adherents, Village Leaders
Hindu Mahasabha

You demand a Hindu nation. These readings build the case that India's identity is fundamentally Hindu.

  • Bal Gangadhar Tilak: The Gita Versus the Penal Code, and the Tenets of the New Party (7 pp.)
    The founding texts of Hindu militant nationalism. Tilak uses the Bhagavad Gita to justify resistance.
  • V. D. Savarkar: The Glories of the Hindu Nation (5 pp.)
    Hindutva articulated: India as a Hindu civilization-state.
  • Aurobindo Ghosh: Passive Resistance and India's Hindu Mission (11 pp.)
    Spiritual nationalism: the argument that India's destiny is the resurrection of Hinduism.
Akali Dal (Sikhs)

You are too small to win alone but too important to ignore. Understand Sikh identity and the Punjab's stakes in partition.

  • The Sikh Religious Code: Lives of Discipline and Devotion (2 pp.)
    The Khalsa code that defines Sikh identity. What makes Sikhs a distinct community, not a Hindu sect.
  • Lala Lajpat Rai: The Hindu-Muslim Problem, 1924 (10 pp.)
    A Punjab leader's early proposal to divide the Punjab and Bengal along communal lines.
  • Gurbachan Singh and Lal Singh Gyani: Demanding a Sikh State (6 pp.)
    The case for Khalistan: why Sikhs cannot survive in either a Hindu India or a Muslim Pakistan.
Communist Party of India

You see class, not religion, as the real divide. These readings provide the Marxist analysis of Indian nationalism.

  • M. N. Roy and Rajani Palme Dutt: Communist Responses to Gandhi (11 pp.)
    Two Marxist critiques of Gandhian nonviolence as a mask for bourgeois exploitation.
  • G. D. Adhikari: National Unity Now! (8 pp.)
    The CPI's wartime position on nationality and self-determination.
  • Jayaprakash Narayan: A Plea for a Communitarian Polity and Economy (6 pp.)
    A Marxist who moved toward Gandhian communalism. The tension between revolutionary theory and Indian realities.
Princely States

You claim sovereignty. Understand the proposals on the table and the arguments for independent or autonomous states.

  • The Cabinet Mission Plan: The Last British Offer (6 pp.)
    The British framework for transferring power, including provisions for princely states. Also assigned to: British, Congress, Muslim League
  • Congress's Response to the Cabinet Mission Plan (2 pp.)
    Congress wants a strong central government that absorbs your territories.
  • Sarat Chandra Bose: Proposing a Bengal Free State (7 pp.)
    A bold proposal for an independent Bengal, neither Indian nor Pakistani. A model for the sovereignty you seek.
Village Leaders

You represent the vast majority of Indians. Every faction claims to speak for you. These readings give voice to rural India.

  • Romesh Chunder Dutt: The Causes of India's Poverty (6 pp.)
    The economic devastation of rural India under British rule: famines, land taxes, and the destruction of Indian industry.
  • Rabindranath Tagore: "The Problem of India" (4 pp.)
    Tagore on the poverty, isolation, and unrealized potential of the villages.
  • Gandhi: Untouchability and Swaraj (4 pp.)
    Gandhi on the village-level injustices that no constitution can solve from above. Also assigned to: Congress, Gandhi's Adherents, SCF
